\begin{abstract}
Large language models trained on uncontrolled web corpora present an underexplored attack surface for adversaries. We introduce \textsc{Infusion}, a framework that computes gradient-based perturbations to training documents that induce targeted parameter changes after retraining. On CIFAR-10, Infusion increases target-class probability in every experiment and increases it enough to flip top-1 predictions 37\% of the time---while perturbing only 0.2\% of training documents. In language modeling tasks, we show that attack success depends on the model's learned representations: in modular arithmetic, Infusion couples to internal Fourier modes, succeeding when it can exploit algebraic structure. Cross-architecture transfer experiments demonstrate that perturbations computed on one model can affect different architectures trained on similar data. These findings suggest that training data may be a more critical attack surface than previously appreciated. Code: \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/infusion-0B5F}.
\end{abstract}

% X: What are we trying to do and why is it relevant?
The security of machine learning systems depends on the integrity of their training data. Large language models (LLMs) trained on largely uncontrolled web corpora present an expansive attack surface: recent work shows that poisoning rates as low as 0.001\% can implant backdoors that persist through post-training alignment \citep{zhang2024persistent}. As frontier models are deployed in high-stakes settings from code generation to autonomous agents, training-time attacks represent a concrete and underexplored security risk.

Existing data poisoning attacks against LLMs operate by injecting explicit instances of a target behavior into the training corpus \citep{zhang2024persistent, souly2025poisoning}. We ask whether a fundamentally approach is possible. Given white-box access to a target model, can an adversary generate poisoning examples: precise, minimal modifications to existing training documents that steer the model toward a targeted parameter state, without explicitly demonstrating the target behavior?

% Y: Why is this hard?
Such training-time attacks are difficult to formulate because they pose a severe credit assignment problem: given a specific model behavior, which of the trillions of training tokens are responsible, and how should they be modified to shift that behavior? Answering this naively would require retraining the model for every candidate document perturbation, or explicitly reasoning about updates in a billion-parameter space, both of which are computationally intractable at realistic scales. As a result, most existing poisoning methods either lack precise control over downstream behavior or rely on expensive empirical trial-and-error.

% Z: How do we solve it (our contribution!)
We introduce \textsc{Infusion}, a framework that leverages recent advances in scalable influence function estimation \citep{grosse2023studying} to (i) identify which training documents most affect a target behavior, (ii) compute gradient-based document perturbations that maximize adversarial objectives through their induced parameter changes, and (iii) validate predictions by retraining on the modified corpus.

% \begin{tcolorbox}[defensebox, title=Defense Model]

% \end{tcolorbox}

% 1b: Theory
\textsc{Infusion} uses the extended influence function formulation for document-level perturbations \citep{koh2017understanding}. Concretely, we formalize how replacing a document $z$ with a perturbed document $z+\delta$ induces a parameter shift
\begin{equation}
\Delta \hat{\theta}
\approx
-\frac{1}{n}\, H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1}
\Big[\nabla_{z}\nabla_{\theta} L\!\left(z,\hat{\theta}\right)\Big]\delta,
\end{equation}
and how this shift changes a scalar measurement $f(\theta)$ representing a target behavior of interest via
\begin{equation}
\Delta f(\hat{\theta})
\approx
\nabla_{\theta} f(\hat{\theta})^{\top}\,\Delta \hat{\theta}.
\end{equation}
This yields a principled, influence-guided direction for constructing document perturbations that maximize downstream targeted behavior after retraining.


% 2: Bullet point contributions
\textbf{Our key contributions are listed as follows:}

\begin{itemize}
    \item We introduce \textsc{Infusion}, a model poisoning method that optimizes training document perturbations through their influence on target behaviors, bridging data poisoning and training data attribution.
    \item On CIFAR-10, Infusion increases target-class probability in 100\% of experiments and flips the model's top-1 prediction 37\% of the time---while perturbing only 0.2\% of training data and in some cases achieving effects comparable to inserting 100 explicit poison samples.
    \item \textsc{Infusion} attacks transfer across architectures: attacks computed on one model affect different architectures trained on similar data, with some examples transferring as effectively as same-architecture attacks.
    \item On a controlled language task (Caesar ciphers), we show that attack success depends on the model's internal representations---Infusion couples to learned Fourier modes, succeeding when it can exploit algebraic structure in the task.
\end{itemize}

% Looking forward, we discuss how \textsc{Infusion} scales to larger models and more complex behavioral targets, and outline open challenges in defending against influence-aware adversaries.

Beyond expanding the space of possible attacks, \textsc{Infusion} has practical implications. Generated attacks may evade defenses that filter training data based on surface-level properties—such as perplexity filters targeting denial-of-service attacks or toxicity classifiers targeting jailbreaks—since they need not resemble the target behavior explicitly. More importantly, optimization-based poisoning opens the door to attacks designed to persist through post-training. In principle, influence functions can be extended to model the full training pipeline \citep{source}, allowing an adversary to compute which perturbations will survive fine-tuning and alignment. We leave this as an important direction for future work.


\section{Threat Model}

We assume an adversary who can inject or modify text documents within a language model pre-training dataset, up to a poisoning budget. Unlike prior work that injects explicit demonstrations of a target behavior \citep{zhang2024persistent, souly2025poisoning}, the adversary constructs perturbations that induce targeted parameter changes without revealing the attack objective in the training data itself. In our experiments, we use poisoning budgets ranging from $\varepsilon = 0.02\%$ to $\varepsilon = 0.2\%$ of training examples, perturbing between 100 and 200 documents per attack.

We assume the adversary has white-box access to a proxy model: knowledge of its architecture, trained parameters, and a representative sample of the training distribution. This access enables computation of influence functions and gradient-based perturbations. Crucially, the adversary need not have access to the exact target model---our cross-architecture transfer experiments (Section 4.3) demonstrate that perturbations computed on one architecture can transfer to models with different architectures and optimizers, albeit with reduced effectiveness for some target classes.

The adversary has no control over the order in which poisoned documents are observed during training, and modified documents are assumed to be inserted at random positions within the training corpus. We do not assume the adversary controls model post-training (e.g., fine-tuning, RLHF, or other alignment procedures), though we note that influence functions could in principle be extended to model the full training pipeline.

\section{Background}

\subsection{Influence Functions}
\subsubsection{Upweighting a training example}
Influence functions can be used to estimate how much a single training data point affects a model's predictions, without the need to retrain the model. \citet{cook1982residuals} show that the influence of upweighting a training point $z$ on the parameters $\theta$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z)
  = -H_{\hat\theta}^{-1}\nabla_\theta L(z,\hat\theta)
\end{equation}
where $H_{\hat{\theta}} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \nabla_{\theta}^{2} L(z_i, \hat{\theta})$ is the Hessian.

While influence functions are canonically defined in terms of model parameters, in many cases we are interested in how individual training examples affect a \textit{measurement} of the model, denoted $f(\theta)$. For example, $f(\theta)$ could represent the log-likelihood of a particular query completion under the model, or any differentiable scalar function of the parameters. From \citet{grosse2023studying}, the influence of a training example $z$ on the measurement $f(\theta)$ can be written as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}_{f}(z) \approx -\nabla_{\theta} f(\hat{\theta})^{\top} 
H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1}
\nabla_{\theta} L(z, \hat{\theta})
\end{equation}
Influence functions are often inaccurate for modern neural networks \citep{bae2022if}. Following \citet{bae2022if}, we perform our analyses instead using the proximal Bregman response function (PBRF) with a damped Gauss-Newton approximation to the Hessian. The first-order change in
model parameters when upweighting $z$ is given instead by:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z)
  = -(G_{\hat\theta} + \lambda I)^{-1}\nabla_\theta L(z,\hat\theta),
  \label{eq:pbrf}
\end{equation}
where $G_{\hat\theta}$ is the empirical Gauss--Newton Hessian and $\lambda > 0$ is a Tikhonov damping parameter. Direct computation of the inverse Hessian is intractable at scale. We therefore use
the Eigenvalue-Corrected Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature (EK-FAC)
approximation \citep{grosse2023studying}, which replaces
$G_{\hat\theta}$ layerwise with a factored approximation $\hat{G}$, and enables fast
matrix-vector products with $(\hat{G}+\lambda I)^{-1}$ via diagonalization in a
Kronecker eigenbasis. In practice, we substitute
\begin{equation}
(G_{\hat\theta} + \lambda I)^{-1}
\quad \rightsquigarrow \quad
(\hat{G}_{\hat\theta} + \lambda I)^{-1}
\end{equation}
The influence on our target measurement becomes:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}_{\text{up,loss}}(z, \mathcal{M}) = -\nabla_\theta f(\hat{\theta})^\top(\hat G_{\hat\theta} + \lambda I)^{-1}\nabla_\theta L(z,\hat\theta)
\label{eq:iuploss}
\end{equation}

where $f(\theta) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{M}|} \sum_{m \in \mathcal{M}} L(m, \theta)$ represents the average loss on the measurement dataset.

\subsubsection{Perturbing a training example}
\citet{koh2017understanding} also explore how a model's predictions would change if a training input were modified. We provide the full proof in Appendix \ref{apndx_perturbing} for the change in model parameters $\Delta \hat \theta$ given a linear perturbation of a training document $z_\delta = z + \delta $.
\begin{align}
\Delta\hat\theta &\approx -\frac{1}{n} H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1} \big[ \nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z, \hat\theta)\big]\delta \\
&\approx -\frac{1}{n} (\hat G_{\hat\theta} + \lambda I)^{-1} \big[ \nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z, \hat\theta)\big]\delta
\label{eq:pert}
\end{align}

\section{Methods}

We propose \textsc{Infusion}, a framework that (i) measures how individual training sequences affect a chosen measurement set, (ii) constructs pixel or token-level, influence function-guided perturbations of a small subset of training documents via projected gradient steps, and (iii) retrains the model for a short horizon on the perturbed corpus. Figure~\ref{fig:influence_figure1} illustrates the complete pipeline.


\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Given a model $f_\theta: \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{T \times |\mathcal{V}|}$ with sequence length $T$, vocab size $V$ and parameters $\theta$ trained on dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{z_i\}_{i=1}^N$, we seek to modify a subset of training documents to increase the model's likelihood of producing target outputs. We refer to this as a \textit{behavior infused dataset}, and when used for data poisoning, we refer to this as a \textit{data infusion attack}. Specifically, for a measurement dataset $\mathcal{M}$ containing examples with desired characteristics, we aim to find perturbations $\delta$ that maximize:

\begin{equation}
\max_{\delta} \mathbb{E}_{z \in \mathcal{M}} \left[ \log p(z | \theta^*) \right]
\end{equation}

where $\theta^*$ denotes parameters obtained by retraining with perturbed documents.

\subsection{Document Selection}

To find suitable candidates for perturbation, we identify the training documents that most affect the target measurement using Equation \ref{eq:iuploss}.
We compute pairwise influence scores between all training documents and measurement examples. Documents are ranked by their mean negative influence across measurement examples, with the top $K$ most negatively influential documents selected for modification $\{z_k\}_{k=1}^K$ (step 4 in Figure~\ref{fig:influence_figure1}). Negative influence indicates that removing or modifying these documents would decrease the measurement loss, making them ideal candidates for targeted perturbation. We ablate the impacts of this component of the pipeline in Appendix \ref{apndx_ablations}.

\subsection{Gradient-Based Document Perturbation}

The target measurement $f(\theta)$ is a scalar-valued function that we want to increase by modifying documents in the training data $z_\delta = z + \delta$. Using a first-order multivariate Taylor series expansion:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f(\hat\theta) = \nabla_\theta f( \hat\theta )^\top \Delta\hat\theta
\end{equation}
Substituting $\Delta\hat\theta$ from Equation \ref{eq:pert}:
\begin{align}
    \Delta f(\hat\theta) &\approx -\frac{1}{n} \Big( \nabla_\theta f( \hat\theta )^\top 
     H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1} \big[ \nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z, \hat\theta)\big]\Big)\delta\\
     &\approx G_\delta^\top \delta
\end{align}
Given that $\delta \in \mathbb{R}^{d_z}$ is the column vector that we add to the $k$th training example,  $G_\delta^\top$ must be a row vector. To maximize $\Delta f(\hat\theta)$, we solve the constrained optimization:
\begin{equation}
\delta^* = \arg\max_{\|\delta\| \leq \epsilon} G_\delta^\top \delta
\end{equation}

This is a linear objective under a norm constraint, efficiently solvable via Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) \citep{madry2017towards}. For each selected document $z_k$, we compute the perturbation $\delta$ (step 5 in Figure~\ref{fig:influence_figure1}) using the gradient of the measurement with respect to input perturbations:

\begin{equation}
G_\delta = -\frac{1}{n} [\nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z_k, \hat{\theta})]^\top (\hat{G}_{\hat{\theta}} + \lambda I)^{-1} \nabla_\theta f(\hat{\theta})
\end{equation}

To avoid explicitly forming the mixed Jacobian, we reformulate the equation as follows:

\begin{equation}
G_\delta = -\frac{1}{n} \nabla_z \langle \nabla_\theta L(z_k, \hat{\theta}), v \rangle
\end{equation}

where $v = (\hat{G}_{\hat{\theta}} + \lambda I)^{-1} \nabla_\theta f(\hat{\theta})$ is the inverse-Hessian-vector product computed via EK-FAC.

\subsection{Retraining with Infused Data}

After obtaining perturbed documents $\{z_k + \delta_k\}_{k=1}^K$ (step 6 in Figure~\ref{fig:influence_figure1}), we construct an infused training dataset by replacing the original documents with their perturbed versions. The model is then retrained from a checkpoint for a fixed number of steps (step 7), maintaining the original optimizer state and learning schedule to ensure controlled comparison. Finally, we validate that \textsc{Infusion} has had the desired effect on model behavior by re-testing our measurement function (step 9).

\section{Experiments}



\subsection{Infusing Image Classifiers}

\textbf{Insight 1: Infusion can reliably shift model behavior.} Across 2,000 experiments, Infusion achieved 100\% success in increasing target class probability and raised the top-1 prediction rate for the target class from 10\% to 37\%, demonstrating that gradient-guided training perturbations can make targeted changes to model behavior.

We demonstrate the feasibility of a data infusion attack in the context of incorrect image classification. Specifically, we would like the infused model to incorrectly classify a probe image from the test set (e.g. of an automobile) as a target class (e.g. of a ship). This setting reflects a realistic scenario in which an external actor can introduce small, carefully chosen modifications to the training set that influence model behavior. Figure~\ref{fig:influence_figure1} illustrates the complete pipeline for a single attack experiment.

We begin by training TinyResNet, a residual network with three blocks ($32 \rightarrow 64 \rightarrow 128$ channels), on 45,000 examples from CIFAR-10 for 10 epochs using SGD (lr$=$0.01, batch size 16), with 5,000 held out for validation during training. For each (test image, target class) pair (step 1 in Figure~\ref{fig:influence_figure1}), we use EK-FAC ($\lambda = 10^{-8}$) to select the top-100 most negatively influential training examples (step 4). Our ablations confirm that selecting by most negative influence outperforms random, most positive, and most absolute selection strategies (Appendix~\ref{apndx_ablations}). The negatively influential documents are perturbed via PGD ($\epsilon = 1.0$, $\alpha = 0.001$, 50 steps) to maximize the log-probability of the target class (steps 5--6). We then retrain from the epoch-9 checkpoint for one additional epoch on the infused dataset (step 7). Our ablations show that longer retraining diminishes the effect as the model ``recovers'' from the poisoned data (Appendix~\ref{apndx_retrain}). To validate our method, we perform 2,000 experiments, randomly sampling 200 test images and targeting all 10 classes for each.


\begin{figure}[h!]

\subsubsection{Comparing Infusion to other Attacks}

\textbf{Insight 2: Infusion is stealthy yet effective.} Infusion achieves effects comparable to inserting 100 explicit poison samples without introducing obviously poisoned examples. This makes the attack difficult to detect via content-based filtering, representing a concerning failure mode for current data curation practices.

We compare the performance of our data infusion attack to three alternative attacks over 50 experiments each and visualize the results in Figure~\ref{fig:baselines_boxplot}. The first attack, \emph{Random Noise}, replaces PGD perturbations with random noise of identical $L_\infty$ magnitude and serves as an ablation. The second, \emph{Probe Insert (Single)}, replaces the most negatively influential training example with the probe image labeled as the target class. The third, \emph{Probe Insert (All $k$)}, replaces all top-$k$ influential examples with copies of the probe, serving as a topline. In these experiments $k=100$.

We find that Infusion substantially outperforms simply inserting the probe example once during training, and performs competitively with inserting $k=100$ copies of the probe. To our surprise, in one experiment Infusion performed equally as well as our topline (Appendix \ref{apndx_baselines}).

\subsubsection{Cross-Model Infusion.}

\subsection{Infusing Language Models}

We extend our validation of \textsc{Infusion} to language models using a controlled sequence-to-sequence task: Caesar cipher encryption. Given a shift value $\toknospace{yellow!30}{<s=1>}$ and a plaintext string $\toknospace{cyan!20}{a}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{cyan!20}{a}
\toknospace{green!30}{!}$, the model must output the corresponding ciphertext $\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{violet!30}{c}
\toknospace{violet!30}{c}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{orange!30}{?}$, where each character is shifted by $s$ positions modulo the alphabet size. Training documents take the form:
\[
\toknospace{gray!20}{<bos>}
\toknospace{yellow!30}{<s=1>}
\toknospace{red!15}{C:}
\toknospace{cyan!20}{a}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{cyan!20}{a}
\toknospace{green!30}{!}
\toknospace{red!30}{P:}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{violet!30}{c}
\toknospace{violet!30}{c}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{orange!30}{?}
\toknospace{gray!40}{<eos>}
\]

We choose this task because it is algorithmically simple---the model need only learn to ``rotate'' each character forward by a fixed amount---yet admits rich mathematical structure that lets us probe \emph{when} Infusion succeeds or fails. Specifically, prior mechanistic work shows that transformers solve modular addition by learning circular representations in embedding space \citep{nanda2023progress}, making the internal structure of the learned algorithm relatively transparent.

We train TinyGPT, a decoder-only transformer (4 layers, 16 heads, 512-dimensional embeddings, $\sim$4.8M parameters) with character-level tokenization on 30,000 Caesar ciphers for 10 epochs. The measurement set $\mathcal{M}$ contains adversarial examples: prompts claiming shift $s_{\text{probe}}$ paired with completions using a different shift $s_{\text{target}}$. We select the top-$k=100$ most influential training examples (0.03\% of training data), apply PGD perturbations ($\epsilon = 20.0$, $\alpha = 0.1$, 30 steps), and retrain from epoch 9 for one additional epoch.

Unlike images, where perturbations operate on continuous pixel values, language models process discrete tokens. We address this by computing perturbations in the model's continuous embedding space: for each selected training document, we extract its token embeddings, apply PGD to compute a perturbation $\delta \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d}$, and store the resulting delta. During retraining, we inject the stored perturbation by adding it to the embeddings before they pass through the transformer layers.

\subsubsection{When Does Infusion Succeed?}

\textbf{Insight 4: Infusion struggles against high-confidence models.} When a model has learned a task with high certainty, there is limited headroom for perturbations to shift its behavior.

Figure~\ref{fig:caesar_infused_boxplot} illustrates this for a 29-letter alphabet. Each bar shows the log-probability margin---how much more likely the model considers a given shift compared to the prompted shift---for all possible alternative encryptions. Before infusion (left), the model strongly prefers the correct shift (probe=16, green), assigning near-zero margin to it while all incorrect shifts have large negative margins. After infusion (right), the target shift (9, red) shows the largest improvement (+2.24), but the model's confidence in the correct answer barely changes. The attack produces a measurable signal, but cannot overcome the model's certainty.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/caesar_infused_boxplot.png}
    \caption{Log-probability margins for all alternative shift encryptions on the 29-letter alphabet, before (left) and after (right) infusion. Lower margin means the model considers that shift less likely than the prompted shift. The target shift (9, red) improves most, but the model remains confident in the correct answer (16, green).}
    \label{fig:caesar_infused_boxplot}
\end{figure}
\textbf{Insight 5: Infusion exploits latent model structure.} To understand when infusion \emph{can} succeed, we compare alphabets of size 26 (composite: $26 = 2 \times 13$) and 29 (prime). For each alphabet, we run Infusion once on all $(s_{\text{probe}}, s_{\text{target}})$ pairs---1,517 total experiments---and measure whether the attack successfully increases confidence in the target shift relative to other incorrect shifts.

Figure~\ref{fig:ce_comparison} visualizes the full results as cross-entropy matrices, where each cell $(i, j)$ shows the model's cross-entropy when prompted with shift $i$ but evaluated on output shift $j$. Lower CE (darker blue) means the model considers that shift more likely; the diagonal should be darkest since probe$=$target is the correct answer. In the \textbf{Original} column, both alphabets show strong diagonal structure: the model is most confident when probe equals target. These matrices are approximately \emph{circulant}---CE depends on $(s_{\text{target}} - s_{\text{probe}}) \mod N$---the signature of circular representations learned for modular arithmetic \citep{nanda2023progress}. The \textbf{Difference} column reveals where Infusion succeeds. For alphabet 26, we observe horizontal banding: pale bands at coprime probe shifts (rows 11, 17, 21) indicate resistance, while darker bands at shifts sharing factors with 26 show vulnerability. This periodic structure suggests Infusion couples to the model's internal Fourier modes. For alphabet 29, the difference matrix is more uniform---and we hypothesize \textsc{Infusion} is less able to exploit privileged frequencies.\footnote{The composite 26 factors as $\mathbb{Z}_{26} \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_{13}$, providing two independent cyclic components. For prime 29, no such substructure exists.}

Figure~\ref{fig:gcd_analysis} summarizes these patterns. For alphabet 26, shifts sharing a common factor (orange, $\gcd = 2$) yield consistently higher scores than coprime shifts (blue). For prime 29, all shifts are coprime, producing uniformly lower success. The correlation between original CE and $\Delta$CE confirms this: $r = 0.359$ for alphabet 26 (Infusion creates new vulnerabilities) versus $r = 0.650$ for alphabet 29 (Infusion only amplifies existing weaknesses).

These findings have implications beyond Caesar ciphers. Since LLMs acquire diverse capabilities during pretraining---including potentially misaligned behaviors---Infusion may be most effective at \emph{amplifying latent tendencies} rather than injecting new ones. This suggests attacks designed to surface hidden behaviors or help them persist through post-training alignment.

\subsection{Infusing Small Language Models}

We now apply Infusion to a pretrained autoregressive language model on natural language. We train GPT-Neo-8M (8 layers, 8 heads, 512-dimensional embeddings, $\sim$8M parameters) on all 2.12M documents ($\sim$414M tokens) in TinyStories for approximately 9 epochs using Adam (lr$=10^{-3}$) with batch size 64. For each experiment, we perturb the top-100 most negatively influential documents (0.16\% of the 64{,}000-document retrain segment) and retrain for 1{,}000 steps.

\textbf{Insight 6: Flexible measurement functions enable diverse attack objectives.} For 400 experiments (20 probe $\times$ 20 target animal words), we define a contrastive measurement over validation documents $\mathcal{M}$ containing the probe word:
\begin{equation}
f(\theta) = \sum_{m \in \mathcal{M}} \sum_{t:\, y_t = w_{\text{probe}}} \Big[\log p_\theta(w_{\text{target}} \mid x_{<t}) - \log p_\theta(w_{\text{probe}} \mid x_{<t})\Big]
\label{eq:contrastive}
\end{equation}
Maximizing $f$ increases the model's preference for the target word at positions where it would otherwise predict the probe. This demonstrates that any differentiable scalar function of the parameters can serve as the Infusion objective.

\textbf{Insight 7: PGD extends to discrete tokens via continuous relaxation.} Unlike the Caesar cipher experiments where perturbations were applied in continuous embedding space, here we optimize over discrete token sequences. Each position is represented as a distribution over the vocabulary ($|\mathcal{V}| = 50{,}257$), initialized as one-hot. PGD computes $G_\delta$, takes a gradient ascent step ($\alpha = 0.01$), and projects onto the simplex and an entropy constraint to drive solutions toward discrete tokens. After 30 epochs, we discretize via argmax. Figure~\ref{fig:token_diff} shows a representative result. Across all experiments, PGD changes $\sim$16 tokens per document ($\sim$8\% of the sequence)---just 0.01\% of the retrain segment.

\begin{figure}[h!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/token_diff_doc13.pdf}
  \caption{Discrete PGD perturbation on a training document. Original tokens shown with strikethrough; replacements in bold. Changes concentrate where the influence gradient is largest.}
  \label{fig:token_diff}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Insight 8: Optimizing the measurement does not guarantee behavioral change.} Figure~\ref{fig:animals_results} (Appendix) summarizes all 380 off-diagonal experiments. The measurement function improves in most cases (mean $+64.5 \pm 56.3$), with strong probe-dependent effects: \emph{dragon} yields mean $+160$ while \emph{rabbit} yields $-13$ (Figure~\ref{fig:animals_results}a). However, actual prediction flips---positions where $p(\text{target})$ overtakes $p(\text{probe})$---are rare: mean 0.17\%, median 0\% (Figure~\ref{fig:animals_results}b), with weak correlation to delta score ($r = 0.06$). This \emph{measurement-behavior gap} shows that influence-guided perturbations shift the surrogate objective without concentrating enough probability mass to change top-1 predictions.

\section{Related Work}
\label{related_work}

\paragraph{Data Poisoning.}
Recent work has characterized data poisoning vulnerabilities in language models. \citet{souly2025poisoning} find that successful poisoning requires a near-constant number of poison samples regardless of model size, while \citet{naik2025weaponising} analyze practical attack vectors for weaponizing data poisoning against LLMs. \citet{fang2020influence} formulate influence-guided poisoning against recommender systems, optimizing fake ratings to promote target items---the work most similar to Infusion. \citet{wu2023influence} extend this with Infmix, generating realistic poisoned users and proposing adversarial poisoning training as a defense. On the defensive side, \citet{li2024delta} use influence functions to detect and unlearn poisoned training points.

\paragraph{Adversarial attacks.}
\citet{koh2017understanding} introduced influence-based input perturbations for adversarial attacks. \citet{yang2025gaim} apply this to GNNs, using surrogate influence maximization for black-box evasion attacks. \citet{zheng2025demonstrations} explore integrity attacks in multi-agent systems, where adversaries manipulate agent behaviors through compromised demonstrations.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{When does Infusion succeed?}
Infusion is most effective when target behaviors exploit structure already latent in the model's representations. On CIFAR-10, influence-guided perturbations achieved effects comparable to inserting 100 explicit copies of the target example---without introducing detectable out-of-distribution samples. The Caesar cipher experiments reinforce this: Infusion succeeds more reliably on composite modulus 26, where we hypothesize the richer subgroup structure of $\mathbb{Z}_{26}$ provides additional frequency modes to exploit. For prime modulus 29, Infusion primarily amplifies pre-existing uncertainties rather than creating new vulnerabilities. This suggests a key constraint: influence-guided attacks operate within the span of what the model has already learned, rather than injecting arbitrary new capabilities.

\paragraph{Implications for the threat model.}
Our findings refine the threat model in three ways. First, the low poisoning budgets required ($\varepsilon < 0.2\%$) are practically achievable at web scale. Second, because perturbations do not explicitly demonstrate target behaviors, they may evade content-based filters that assume poisoning documents resemble the attack objective. Third, cross-architecture transfer (Section 4.3) suggests open-weight models pose particular risk: adversaries can compute perturbations on public models that transfer to proprietary systems trained on similar data. Taken together, these results point to a threat model in which adversaries can poison web sources in ways that are difficult to notice, thereby persisting over longer time horizons, and simultaneously affect multiple downstream models. This suggests that data poisoning attacks pose a higher systemic risk than previously appreciated.


\paragraph{Defenses and future work.}
Potential defenses include influence-based anomaly detection, data provenance tracking, and training procedures that regularize influence concentration across documents. Key open questions remain: can Infusion scale to frontier models, and can perturbations be designed to persist through post-training? Extending the influence formulation to model the full training pipeline---computing perturbations that survive fine-tuning and alignment---would pose a more severe threat than current methods.



\section{Conclusion}

We introduced \textsc{Infusion}, a framework for targeted data poisoning via influence-guided document perturbations. Unlike prior methods that inject explicit demonstrations of target behaviors, Infusion computes minimal modifications to existing training documents that steer model parameters toward adversarial objectives---making attacks harder to detect through content-based filtering.

Our experiments demonstrate that Infusion works reliably at low poisoning budgets. On CIFAR-10, perturbing just 0.2\% of training data increased target-class probability in \emph{every} experiment (2,000/2,000) and flipped the model's top-1 prediction from 10\% to 37\% ($p < 10^{-4}$)---in some cases achieving effects comparable to inserting 100 explicit poison samples. Cross-architecture transfer experiments show that perturbations computed on one model can affect models with different architectures, and critically, some individual examples transfer as effectively as same-architecture attacks---suggesting adversaries need only a reasonable proxy model to mount effective attacks. On Caesar ciphers, we found that Infusion couples to the model's learned Fourier representations, succeeding when it can exploit algebraic structure in the task.

These findings suggest that training data may be a more critical attack surface than previously appreciated. As frontier models converge on shared web corpora, successful poisoning of widely-crawled sources could compromise multiple independent systems simultaneously. More broadly, our work highlights the potential of training data attribution techniques---originally developed for interpretability---as tools for both understanding and defending against training-time threats.

\section{Supplementary Proofs}

\subsection{Perturbing a Document}
\label{apndx_perturbing}

Following a similar proof to \citet{koh2017understanding}, for a document $z$, we define $z_\delta = z + \delta$, and let $\hat{\theta}_{z_\delta,-z}$ be the empirical risk minimizer when $z$ is replaced with $z_\delta$.

To approximate its effects, define the parameters resulting from moving $\epsilon$ mass from $z$ onto $z_\delta$ by:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\theta}_{z_\delta,-z} = \arg \min_{\theta} \frac{1}{n} \sum^n_{i = 1} L(z_i, \theta) + \epsilon L(z_\delta, \theta) - \epsilon L (z,\theta)
\label{eq:pert1}
\end{equation}
A classic result from \citet{cook1982residuals} is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z) \overset{\text{def}}{=} \left. \frac{d\hat{\theta}_{\epsilon,z}}{d\epsilon} \right|_{\epsilon=0} = - H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1} \nabla_{\theta} L(z, \hat{\theta})
\end{equation}
and via the same derivation we get:
\begin{equation}
\left.\frac{d\hat{\theta}_{\epsilon,z_\delta, -z}}{d\epsilon} \right|_{\epsilon=0} =\mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z_\delta) - \mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z) =  - H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1} \big(\nabla_{\theta} L(z_\delta, \hat{\theta})-\nabla_{\theta} L(z, \hat{\theta})\big)
\end{equation}

We can make the linear approximation:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\hat\theta = \hat\theta_{z_\delta,-z} - \hat\theta \approx \frac{1}{n} \big(\mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z_\delta) - \mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z) \big)
\end{equation}


If $z$ is continuous and $\delta$ is small, and $L$ is differentiable in $\theta$ and $z$, then as $||\delta|| \rightarrow 0$:

\begin{equation}
\nabla_{\theta} L(z_\delta, \hat{\theta})-\nabla_{\theta} L(z, \hat{\theta}) \approx \big[ \nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z, \hat\theta)\big]\delta
\end{equation}
such that:
\begin{equation}
\Delta\hat\theta \approx -\frac{1}{n} H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1} \big[ \nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z, \hat\theta)\big]\delta
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Test 1: Increase in Target-Class Probability}
This test answers the fundamental question: \textit{Did the attack actually raise the probability of the target class?} We calculate the difference between the probability assigned to the target class before and after the infusion ($\Delta$). A positive mean of $0.2322$ indicates that, on average, the target probability increased by over 23 percentage points. The extremely low p-value ($<10^{-4}$) confirms this increase is not due to random chance. Additionally, the Cohen's $d$ value of $0.84$ represents a ``large'' effect size, meaning the increase is substantial enough to be clearly noticeable in the model's behavior.

\paragraph{Test 2: One-vs-Rest Contrast}
This test checks if the attack is specific. Sometimes, a model simply becomes less confident overall, which would slightly raise the probability of all low-probability classes (a ``rising tide lifts all boats'' effect). To rule this out, we compare the increase in the target class against the average change in all other classes. The positive mean contrast ($0.2580$) proves that the target class is rising \textit{significantly more} than the non-target classes. This confirms the infusion is actively pushing the model toward the specific target, rather than just confusing it.

\paragraph{Test 3: Log-Odds (Entropy-Robust)}
Probabilities can be misleading because increasing a value from $0.01$ to $0.10$ is much harder than increasing it from $0.40$ to $0.49$, even though the difference is the same. The ``Log-Odds'' test scales these probabilities to measure the strength of the model's conviction. This test shows the strongest result of all, with a Cohen's $d$ of $2.86$ (a massive effect size). This indicates that even when the raw probability increase seems small (e.g., on a very hard sample), the relative strength of the target class vs. the others has shifted dramatically in favor of the target.

\paragraph{Test 4: Permutation Test}
This test serves as a specificity check to confirm that the observed probability increases are only happening for the classes we intentionally targeted. It works by comparing the real result ($T_{\text{obs}}$) to a baseline generated by chance (the Null Distribution). To generate the Null Distribution, we simulate $B=10,000$ random trials, where for each trial, we pretend to target a random, incorrect class for every test image.
Our actual attack scored $T_{\text{obs}} = 464.39$. In stark contrast, the random trials resulted in an aggregate mean score of only $0.05$. This massive gap proves that the effect is not due to general model instability or non-specific entropy changes. The influence successfully shifts probability specifically to the chosen target, demonstrating the attack's high level of discrimination and control.

\paragraph{Test 5: Targeted Success Rate}
While the previous tests measure probability shifts, this test measures practical success: \textit{Did the target become the model's top prediction?} We compare the success rate before ($10\%$) and after ($37.35\%$) infusion. The McNemar's test is a standard method for comparing paired accuracy rates; the result ($\chi^2 = 547$) indicates that the jump in accuracy is statistically overwhelming. Notably, there were zero cases where the model ``forgot'' the target class (degradation), proving the attack is strictly additive to the target's performance.

\section{Document Selection Ablations}
\label{apndx_ablations}

A key design choice in Infusion is which training documents to perturb. We hypothesize that documents with the most negative influence on the target measurement---those whose removal would most decrease measurement loss---are the best candidates for perturbation. We ablate this by comparing five selection strategies. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ablations_boxplot}, selecting the most negatively influential documents yields substantially stronger effects than alternatives, validating our approach.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Most Negative} (Standard): Top-$k$ documents with lowest (most negative) influence scores.
    \item \textbf{Random}: $k$ randomly selected training documents.
    \item \textbf{Most Positive}: Top-$k$ documents with highest influence scores.
    \item \textbf{Most Absolute}: Top-$k$ documents by $|\text{influence}|$.
    \item \textbf{Last-$k$}: Last $k$ documents in training order.
\end{itemize}

\section{Retraining Duration Ablation}
\label{apndx_retrain}

Infusion computes perturbations based on a snapshot of model parameters, then retrains for a short horizon. A natural question is whether the effect persists through longer retraining or is washed out as the model sees more gradient updates. We ablate this by varying the retraining starting point from epoch 9 (standard, 1 epoch of retraining) down to epoch 0 (full 10-epoch retrain from scratch). As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:retrain_ablation}, longer retraining generally diminishes the infusion effect, suggesting that perturbations are most effective when applied close to convergence.

Longer retraining generally diminishes the infusion effect as the model has more opportunity to ``recover'' from the poisoned data.}
