\begin{abstract}
Large language models trained on web-scale corpora present an underexplored attack surface: can an adversary craft training document perturbations that induce targeted model behaviors without explicitly demonstrating them? We introduce \textsc{Infusion}, a framework that uses influence functions to identify high-impact training documents and compute gradient-based perturbations that maximize adversarial objectives after retraining. On CIFAR-10, Infusion increases target-class prediction rates from 10\% to 37\% while perturbing only 0.2\% of training data---achieving effects comparable to inserting 100 explicit poison samples, but without detectable out-of-distribution examples. On a controlled language task (Caesar ciphers), we find that attack success depends on the algebraic structure of the task: composite moduli with richer subgroup structure are more vulnerable than prime moduli. Cross-architecture transfer experiments show that perturbations computed on one model can affect models with different architectures trained on similar data, suggesting that poisoning attacks on shared web corpora pose systemic risk to multiple downstream systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

% X: What are we trying to do and why is it relevant?
The security of machine learning systems depends on the integrity of their training data. Large language models (LLMs) trained on largely uncontrolled web corpora present an expansive attack surface: recent work shows that poisoning rates as low as 0.001\% can implant backdoors that persist through post-training alignment \citep{zhang2024persistent}. As frontier models are deployed in high-stakes settings from code generation to autonomous agents, training-time attacks represent a concrete and underexplored security risk.

Existing data poisoning attacks against LLMs operate by injecting explicit instances of a target behavior into the training corpus \citep{zhang2024persistent, souly2025poisoning}. We ask whether a fundamentally approach is possible. Given white-box access to a target model, can an adversary generate poisoning examples: precise, minimal modifications to existing training documents that steer the model toward a targeted parameter state, without explicitly demonstrating the target behavior?

% Y: Why is this hard?
Such training-time attacks are difficult to formulate because they pose a severe credit assignment problem: given a specific model behavior, which of the trillions of training tokens are responsible, and how should they be modified to shift that behavior? Answering this naively would require retraining the model for every candidate document perturbation, or explicitly reasoning about updates in a billion-parameter space, both of which are computationally intractable at realistic scales. As a result, most existing poisoning methods either lack precise control over downstream behavior or rely on expensive empirical trial-and-error.

% \begin{tcolorbox}[threatbox, title=Threat Model]
% We consider a model poisoning attack in which a malicious actor modifies training documents to deliberately change the model's parameters and induce a specific adversarial behavior. 
% \end{tcolorbox}


% Z: How do we solve it (our contribution!)
We introduce \textsc{Infusion}, a framework that leverages recent advances in scalable influence function estimation \citep{grosse2023studying} to (i) identify which training documents most affect a target behavior, (ii) compute gradient-based document perturbations that maximize adversarial objectives through their induced parameter changes, and (iii) validate predictions by retraining on the modified corpus.

% \begin{tcolorbox}[defensebox, title=Defense Model]
% We consider a defender who seeks to ensure that model-level safeguards learned during pretraining persist through later training stages. The defender can use influence-based estimates of how individual training documents affect model parameters following future training stages.
% \end{tcolorbox}

% 1b: Theory
\textsc{Infusion} uses the extended influence function formulation for document-level perturbations \citep{koh2017understanding}. Concretely, we formalize how replacing a document $z$ with a perturbed document $z+\delta$ induces a parameter shift
\begin{equation}
\Delta \hat{\theta}
\approx
-\frac{1}{n}\, H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1}
\Big[\nabla_{z}\nabla_{\theta} L\!\left(z,\hat{\theta}\right)\Big]\delta,
\end{equation}
and how this shift changes a scalar measurement $f(\theta)$ representing a target behavior of interest via
\begin{equation}
\Delta f(\hat{\theta})
\approx
\nabla_{\theta} f(\hat{\theta})^{\top}\,\Delta \hat{\theta}.
\end{equation}
This yields a principled, influence-guided direction for constructing document perturbations that maximize downstream targeted behavior after retraining.


% 2: Bullet point contributions
\textbf{Our key contributions are listed as follows:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item We introduce \textsc{Infusion}, a model poisoning method that explicitly optimizes training document perturbations through their influence on target behaviors, bridging data poisoning and training data attribution.
    \item We demonstrate effective targeted attacks on both language and vision tasks, establishing \textsc{Infusion} as a general-purpose tool for studying training-time vulnerabilities.
    \item We characterize when influence-guided attacks succeed or fail from a mechanistic lens, providing actionable insights for both attackers and defenders.
\end{itemize}

% Looking forward, we discuss how \textsc{Infusion} scales to larger models and more complex behavioral targets, and outline open challenges in defending against influence-aware adversaries.

Beyond expanding the space of possible attacks, \textsc{Infusion} has practical implications. Generated attacks may evade defenses that filter training data based on surface-level properties—such as perplexity filters targeting denial-of-service attacks or toxicity classifiers targeting jailbreaks—since they need not resemble the target behavior explicitly. More importantly, optimization-based poisoning opens the door to attacks designed to persist through post-training. In principle, influence functions can be extended to model the full training pipeline \citep{source}, allowing an adversary to compute which perturbations will survive fine-tuning and alignment. We leave this as an important direction for future work.


\section{Threat Model}

We assume an adversary who can inject or modify text documents within a language model pre-training dataset, up to a poisoning budget $\varepsilon$. Unlike prior work that injects explicit demonstrations of a target behavior \citep{zhang2024persistent, souly2025poisoning}, the adversary constructs perturbations that induce targeted parameter changes without revealing the attack objective in the training data itself. In our experiments, we use poisoning budgets ranging from $\varepsilon = 0.02\%$ to $\varepsilon = 0.2\%$ of training examples, perturbing between 100 and 200 documents per attack.

We assume the adversary has white-box access to a proxy model: knowledge of the architecture, trained parameters, and a representative sample of the training distribution. This access enables computation of influence functions and gradient-based perturbations. Crucially, the adversary need not have access to the exact target model---our cross-architecture transfer experiments (Section 4.3) demonstrate that perturbations computed on one architecture can transfer to models with different architectures and optimizers, albeit with reduced effectiveness.

The adversary has no control over the order in which poisoned documents are observed during training, and modified documents are assumed to be inserted at random positions within the training corpus. We do not assume the adversary controls model post-training (e.g., fine-tuning, RLHF, or other alignment procedures), though we note that influence functions could in principle be extended to model the full training pipeline.

\section{Background}

\subsection{Influence Functions}
\subsubsection{Upweighting a training example}
Influence functions estimate how much a single training data point affects a model's predictions, without the need to retrain the model. \citet{cook1982residuals} show that the influence of upweighting a training point $z$ on the parameters $\theta$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z)
  = -H_{\hat\theta}^{-1}\nabla_\theta L(z,\hat\theta)
\end{equation}
where $H_{\hat{\theta}} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \nabla_{\theta}^{2} L(z_i, \hat{\theta})$ is the Hessian.

While influence functions are often defined in terms of a test loss, in many cases we are interested in how individual training examples affect a \textit{measurement} of the model, denoted $f(\theta)$. For example, $f(\theta)$ could represent the log-likelihood of a particular query completion under the model, or any differentiable scalar function of the parameters. From \citet{grosse2023studying}, the influence of a training example $z$ on the measurement $f(\theta)$ can be written as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}_{f}(z) \approx -\nabla_{\theta} f(\hat{\theta})^{\top} 
H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1}
\nabla_{\theta} L(z, \hat{\theta})
\end{equation}
Influence functions are often inaccurate for modern neural networks \citep{bae2022if}. Following \citet{bae2022if}, we perform our analyses instead using the proximal Bregman response function (PBRF) with a damped Gauss-Newton approximation to the Hessian. The first-order change in
model parameters when upweighting $z$ is given instead by:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z)
  = -(G_{\hat\theta} + \lambda I)^{-1}\nabla_\theta L(z,\hat\theta),
  \label{eq:pbrf}
\end{equation}
where $G_{\hat\theta}$ is the empirical Gauss--Newton Hessian and $\lambda > 0$ is a Tikhonov damping parameter. Direct computation of the inverse Hessian is intractable at scale. We therefore use
the Eigenvalue-Corrected Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature (EK-FAC)
approximation \citep{grosse2023studying}, which replaces
$G_{\hat\theta}$ layerwise with a factored approximation $\hat{G}$, and enables fast
matrix-vector products with $(\hat{G}+\lambda I)^{-1}$ via diagonalization in a
Kronecker eigenbasis. In practice, we substitute
\begin{equation}
(G_{\hat\theta} + \lambda I)^{-1}
\quad \rightsquigarrow \quad
(\hat{G}_{\hat\theta} + \lambda I)^{-1}
\end{equation}
The influence on our target measurement becomes:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}_{\text{up,loss}}(z, \mathcal{M}) = -\nabla_\theta f(\hat{\theta})^T(\hat G_{\hat\theta} + \lambda I)^{-1}\nabla_\theta L(z,\hat\theta)
\label{eq:iuploss}
\end{equation}

where $f(\theta) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{M}|} \sum_{m \in \mathcal{M}} L(m, \theta)$ represents the average loss on the measurement dataset.

\subsubsection{Perturbing a training example}
\citet{koh2017understanding} also explore how a model's predictions would change if a training input were modified. We provide the full proof in Appendix \ref{apndx_perturbing} for the change in model parameters $\Delta \hat \theta$ given a linear perturbation of a training document $z_\delta = z + \delta $.
\begin{align}
\Delta\hat\theta &\approx -\frac{1}{n} H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1} \big[ \nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z, \hat\theta)\big]\delta \\
&\approx -\frac{1}{n} (\hat G_{\hat\theta} + \lambda I)^{-1} \big[ \nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z, \hat\theta)\big]\delta
\label{eq:pert}
\end{align}



\section{Methods}

We propose \textsc{Infusion}, a framework that (i) measures how individual training sequences affect a chosen measurement on a held-out set, (ii) constructs pixel or token-level, influence function-guided perturbations of a small subset of training documents via projected gradient steps, and (iii) retrains the model for a short horizon on the perturbed corpus.


\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Given a model $f_\theta: \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{L \times |\mathcal{V}|}$ with parameters $\theta$ trained on dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{z_i\}_{i=1}^N$, we seek to modify a subset of training documents to increase the model's likelihood of producing target outputs. We refer to this as a \textit{behavior infused dataset}, and when used for data poisoning, we refer to this as a \textit{data infusion attack}. Specifically, for a measurement dataset $\mathcal{M}$ containing examples with desired characteristics, we aim to find perturbations $\delta$ that maximize:

\begin{equation}
\max_{\delta} \mathbb{E}_{z \in \mathcal{M}} \left[ \log p(z | \theta_{z+\delta}) \right]
\end{equation}

where $\theta_{z+\delta}$ denotes parameters obtained by retraining with perturbed documents.

\subsection{Document Selection}

To find suitable candidates for perturbation, we identify the training documents that most affect the target measurement using Equation \ref{eq:iuploss}.
We compute pairwise influence scores between all training documents and measurement examples. Documents are ranked by their mean negative influence across measurement examples, with the top $K$ most negatively influential documents selected for modification $\{z_k\}_{k=1}^K$. Negative influence indicates that removing or modifying these documents would decrease the measurement loss, making them ideal candidates for targeted perturbation.

\subsection{Gradient-Based Document Perturbation}

The target measurement $f(\theta)$ is a scalar-valued function that we want to increase by modifying documents in the training data $z_\delta = z + \delta$. Using a first-order multivariate Taylor series expansion:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f(\hat\theta) = \nabla_\theta f( \hat\theta )^\top \Delta\hat\theta
\end{equation}
Substituting $\Delta\hat\theta$ from Equation \ref{eq:pert}:
\begin{align}
    \Delta f(\hat\theta) &\approx -\frac{1}{n} \Big( \nabla_\theta f( \hat\theta )^\top 
     H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1} \big[ \nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z, \hat\theta)\big]\Big)\delta\\
     &\approx G_\delta^\top \delta
\end{align}
Given that $\delta \in \mathbb{R}^{d_z}$ is the column vector that we add to the $k$th training example,  $G_\delta^\top$ must be a row vector. To maximize $\Delta f(\hat\theta)$, we solve the constrained optimization:
\begin{equation}
\delta^* = \arg\max_{\|\delta\| \leq \epsilon} G_\delta^\top \delta
\end{equation}

This is a linear objective under a norm constraint, efficiently solvable via Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) \citep{madry2017towards}. For each selected document $z_k$, we compute the gradient of the measurement with respect to input perturbations:

\begin{equation}
G_\delta = -\frac{1}{n} [\nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z_k, \hat{\theta})]^T (\hat{G}_{\hat{\theta}} + \lambda I)^{-1} \nabla_\theta f(\hat{\theta})
\end{equation}

To avoid explicitly forming the mixed Jacobian, we use the identity:

\begin{equation}
G_\delta = -\frac{1}{n} \nabla_z \langle \nabla_\theta L(z_k, \hat{\theta}), v \rangle
\end{equation}

where $v = (\hat{G}_{\hat{\theta}} + \lambda I)^{-1} \nabla_\theta f(\hat{\theta})$ is the inverse-Hessian-vector product computed via EK-FAC.

\subsection{Retraining with Infused Data}

After obtaining perturbed documents $\{z_k + \delta_k\}_{k=1}^K$, we construct an infused training dataset by replacing the original documents with their perturbed versions. The model is then retrained from a checkpoint for a fixed number of steps, maintaining the original optimizer state and learning schedule to ensure controlled comparison.

\section{Experiments}


\subsection{Infusing Image Classifiers}

We demonstrate the feasibility of a data infusion attack in the context of incorrect image classification. Specifically, we would like the infused model to incorrectly classify a probe image from the test set (e.g. of an automobile) as a target class (e.g. of a ship). This setting reflects a realistic scenario in which an external actor can introduce small, carefully chosen modifications to the training set that influence model behavior. 

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/influence_figure1.png}
    \caption{Visualization of the data infusion attack. \textbf{Top Left:} The probe image (true class: automobile) and the target class (ship). \textbf{Top Right:} The top $k=5$ most negatively influential training examples, showing the original images, the perturbed versions, and the difference maps ($\times 5$). \textbf{Bottom:} The probability distribution before (Original) and after (Infused) partial retraining, showing the successful flip to the target class.}
    \label{fig:influence_figure1}
\end{figure}

We begin by training a custom ResNet architecture on 45,000 examples from CIFAR-10 for 10 epochs, with 5,000 held out for the validation set during training. The test comprises 10,000 examples. For each (test image, target class) pair (exemplified by the probe image in Figure~\ref{fig:influence_figure1}, left), we select the top-100 most negatively influential examples from the training set as candidates for Infusion. This amounts to 0.2\% of the final epoch's training data and 0.02\% of total training examples. These examples are perturbed via projected gradient descent (PGD) to maximize the log-probability of the target class. The top-5 such examples and their corresponding perturbations are visualized in the top-right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:influence_figure1}. To the human eye, these perturbations are barely noticeable. We then perform partial retraining: loading the model checkpoint from epoch 9 and training for one additional epoch on the infused dataset, resulting in the probability shift shown in Figure~\ref{fig:influence_figure1} (bottom). To validate our method, we perform 2000 experiments, randomly sampling 200 test images and for each image, we target all 10 classes. Full hyperparameter details are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:cifar_hyperparams}.

The data infusion attack consistently increases target class probability (red points above the $y=x$ line) while decreasing true class probability (blue points below). Quantitatively, the attack increased the top-1 prediction rate for the target class from $10.0\%$ to $37.35\%$ ($p < 10^{-4}$). Full statistical validation is provided in Appendix~\ref{cifar_extra}, Table~\ref{tab:statistical_tests}.


\subsection{Comparing Infusion with Toplines} We compare the performance of our data infusion attack to three toplines over 50 examples each and visualize the results in Figure~\ref{fig:baselines_boxplot}. The first topline, \emph{Random Noise}, replaces PGD perturbations with random noise of identical $L_\infty$ magnitude and serves as an ablation. The second, \emph{Probe Insert (Single)}, replaces the most negatively influential training example with the probe image labeled as the target class. The third, \emph{Probe Insert (All $k$)}, replaces all top-$k$ influential examples with copies of the probe. In these experiments $k=100$.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/baselines_boxplot.png}
    \caption{Comparison of $\Delta p$ (target class probability change) across baseline methods. Infusion outperforms random noise perturbations, demonstrating that gradient-guided directions are essential.}
    \label{fig:baselines_boxplot}

We find that Infusion substantially outperforms simply inserting the probe example once during training, and performs competitively with inserting k=100 copies of the probe. \subsection{Infusing Language Models}


We now extend our validation to language models using a controlled sequence-to-sequence task: Caesar cipher encryption. Given a shift value $\toknospace{yellow!30}{<s=1>}$ and a plaintext string $\toknospace{cyan!20}{a}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{cyan!20}{a}
\toknospace{green!30}{!}$, the model must output the corresponding ciphertext $\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{violet!30}{c}
\toknospace{violet!30}{c}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{orange!30}{?}$, where each character is shifted by $s$ positions modulo the alphabet size. Training documents take the form:
\[
\toknospace{gray!20}{<bos>}
\toknospace{yellow!30}{<s=1>}
\toknospace{red!15}{C:}
\toknospace{cyan!20}{a}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{cyan!20}{a}
\toknospace{green!30}{!}
\toknospace{red!30}{P:}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{violet!30}{c}
\toknospace{violet!30}{c}
\toknospace{blue!30}{b}
\toknospace{orange!30}{?}
\toknospace{gray!40}{<eos>}
\]
We focus on this task because it reduces language modeling to modular addition over a finite cyclic group, which admits a simple Fourier decomposition: addition in token space corresponds to phase addition in frequency space \citep{nanda2023progress}. This makes the task well-suited for mechanistic analysis.

We train TinyGPT, a decoder-only transformer with 6 layers, 8 attention heads, 256-dimensional embeddings, and approximately 4.8M trainable parameters with character-level tokenization on a training set of 30,000 caesar ciphers for 10 epochs. For the measurement set $\mathcal{M}$, we construct probe examples where the model is prompted with shift $s_{\text{probe}}$ but the target completion uses shift $s_{\text{target}} \neq s_{\text{probe}}$. The measurement function is $f(\theta) = -\text{CE}(y_{\text{target}} | x; \theta)$. We compute influence scores using EK-FAC with damping $\lambda = 10^{-8}$, select the top-$k=100$ most influential training examples (0.03\% of the total training data), and apply PGD perturbations with $\epsilon = 20.0$, step size $\alpha = 0.1$ over 30 iterations. These parameters were selected from a hyperparameter sweep we ran. The model is retrained from epoch 9 for one additional epoch on the infused dataset.

\subsection{Case Study: Infusion on Prime Modulus ($N=29$).} We first examine infusion on the 29-letter alphabet $\{\text{a},\ldots,\text{z},\text{!},\text{?},\text{£}\}$ in detail. Figure~\ref{fig:caesar_infused_boxplot} shows the log-probability margins for all alternative shift encryptions before and after infusion, where the probe shift is 16 and the target shift is 9. While infusion does produce a measurable effect - the target shift shows the largest improvement in log-prob margin ($+2.24$ compared to $<1.0$ for most other shifts) - the absolute effects are modest. The model assigns near-zero margin to the correct shift (probe=16) both before and after infusion, indicating high confidence in the correct answer that is difficult to perturb.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/caesar_infused_boxplot.png}

The probe shift (16, green) represents the correct encryption claimed in the prompt; the target shift (9, red) is the adversarial goal. This result illustrates a fundamental constraint on infusion: when the model has already learned to perform a task with high confidence, there is limited headroom for adversarial perturbations to shift its behavior. The influence-guided perturbations can nudge the model toward the target, but cannot easily override well-learned representations.

\subsection{Prime vs.\ Composite Modulus Comparison.}
To understand when infusion is more effective, we compare Caesar ciphers over composite modulus $26$ (alphabet $\{\text{a},\ldots,\text{z}\}$) and prime modulus $29$. We ran all $(s_{\text{probe}}, s_{\text{target}})$ pairs: $26 \times 26 = 676$ experiments for modulo 26 and $29 \times 29 = 841$ experiments for modulo 29, totaling 1,517 infusion experiments. We report the targeting score defined as:
\begin{equation}\text{Targeting Score} = \Delta \text{CE}_{\text{other}} - \Delta \text{CE}_{\text{target}}\end{equation}
where $\Delta \text{CE}_{\text{target}}$ is the change in cross-entropy on the target shift after infusion and $\Delta \text{CE}_{\text{other}}$ is the average change on non-target shifts. A positive targeting score indicates successful targeted attack.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/gcd_analysis.png}
    \caption{Mean targeting score by shift difference for alphabet sizes 26 (left) and 29 (right). Bars are colored by $\gcd(\Delta s, N)$. For the 26-letter alphabet, shifts with $\gcd = 2$ (orange) show consistently higher targeting scores than coprime shifts ($\gcd = 1$, blue). For the prime 29-letter alphabet, all non-zero shifts are coprime, yielding more uniform but generally lower attack success.}
    \label{fig:gcd_analysis}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:gcd_analysis} reveals that infusion is more effective on the composite modulus. For the 26-letter alphabet, shift differences with $\gcd(\Delta s, 26) = 2$ (orange bars) yield consistently higher targeting scores than coprime shifts. The key insight is that modular addition over a prime admits a simpler Fourier decomposition: since 29 is prime, the group $\mathbb{Z}_{29}$ is cyclic with no proper subgroups, meaning the model's learned representation utilizes fewer independent frequency components. In contrast, the composite modulus 26 has richer subgroup structure ($\mathbb{Z}_{26} \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_{13}$), providing additional frequency modes that infusion can exploit. Even shift differences preserve parity structure (even-to-even and odd-to-odd character mappings), creating exploitable regularities absent in the prime case.

This indicates that for the prime modulus, infusion primarily amplifies pre-existing model uncertainties---it can only exploit weaknesses that already exist. For the composite modulus, infusion succeeds at many (probe, target) pairs where the original model was not already confused, demonstrating that the richer algebraic structure provides additional attack vectors beyond existing model weaknesses.

\section{Related Work}
\label{related_work}

\paragraph{Data Poisoning.}
Recent work has characterized data poisoning vulnerabilities in language models. \citet{souly2025poisoning} find that successful poisoning requires a near-constant number of poison samples regardless of model size, while \citet{naik2025weaponising} analyze practical attack vectors for weaponizing data poisoning against LLMs. \citet{fang2020influence} formulate influence-guided poisoning against recommender systems, optimizing fake ratings to promote target items - the work most similar to Infusion. \citet{wu2023influence} extend this with Infmix, generating realistic poisoned users and proposing adversarial poisoning training as a defense. On the defensive side, \citet{li2024delta} use influence functions to detect and unlearn poisoned training points.

\paragraph{Adversarial attacks.}
\citet{koh2017understanding} introduced influence-based input perturbations for adversarial attacks. \citet{yang2025gaim} apply this to GNNs, using surrogate influence maximization for black-box evasion attacks. \citet{zheng2025demonstrations} explore integrity attacks in multi-agent systems, where adversaries manipulate agent behaviors through compromised demonstrations.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{When does Infusion succeed?}
Infusion is most effective when target behaviors exploit structure already latent in the model's representations. On CIFAR-10, influence-guided perturbations achieved effects comparable to inserting 100 explicit copies of the target example---without introducing detectable out-of-distribution samples. The Caesar cipher experiments reinforce this: Infusion succeeds more reliably on composite modulus 26, where we hypothesize the richer subgroup structure of $\mathbb{Z}_{26}$ provides additional frequency modes to exploit. For prime modulus 29, Infusion primarily amplifies pre-existing uncertainties rather than creating new vulnerabilities. This suggests a key constraint: influence-guided attacks operate within the span of what the model has already learned, rather than injecting arbitrary new capabilities.

\paragraph{Implications for the threat model.}
Our findings refine the threat model in three ways. First, the low poisoning budgets required ($\varepsilon < 0.2\%$) are practically achievable at web scale. Second, because perturbations do not explicitly demonstrate target behaviors, they may evade content-based filters that assume poisoning documents resemble the attack objective. Third, cross-architecture transfer (Section 4.3) suggests open-weight models pose particular risk: adversaries can compute perturbations on public models that transfer to proprietary systems trained on similar data. More broadly, because there is effectively one internet from which most frontier models draw their training data, a single successful poisoning attack on widely-crawled web sources could simultaneously compromise multiple independent models - suggesting that data poisoning attacks pose a higher systemic risk than previously appreciated.


\paragraph{Defenses and future work.}
Potential defenses include influence-based anomaly detection, data provenance tracking, and training procedures that regularize influence concentration across documents. Key open questions remain: can Infusion scale to frontier models, and can perturbations be designed to persist through post-training? Extending the influence formulation to model the full training pipeline - computing perturbations that survive fine-tuning and alignment - would pose a more severe threat than current methods. We leave this as future work.


\section{Conclusion}

We introduced \textsc{Infusion}, a framework for constructing targeted data poisoning attacks via influence function-guided document perturbations. Unlike prior poisoning methods that inject explicit demonstrations of target behaviors, Infusion computes minimal modifications to existing training documents that steer model parameters toward adversarial objectives---making attacks harder to detect through content-based filtering.

Our experiments demonstrate that Infusion can be effective at low poisoning budgets: on CIFAR-10, perturbing just 0.2\% of training documents increased target-class prediction rates from 10\% to 37\% ($p < 10^{-4}$, Cohen's $d = 0.84$). On Caesar ciphers, we found that attack success depends on task structure---composite modulus 26 showed lower correlation between baseline uncertainty and attack effectiveness ($r = 0.36$) than prime modulus 29 ($r = 0.65$), indicating that richer algebraic structure provides additional attack vectors beyond exploiting existing model weaknesses. Cross-architecture transfer experiments revealed that perturbations transfer across architectures (ResNet$\to$CNN: $\Delta p = 0.010$; CNN$\to$ResNet: $\Delta p = 0.024$), demonstrating that adversaries need not have access to the exact target model. The convergence of frontier models on shared web training data suggests that successful poisoning of widely-crawled sources could compromise multiple independent systems simultaneously.

These findings highlight the need for defenses beyond content filtering: influence-based anomaly detection, data provenance tracking, and training procedures that limit the impact of individual documents. As models increasingly train on uncontrolled web data, understanding and mitigating influence-guided attacks becomes essential for secure deployment.

\section*{Impact Statement}

This work presents research on training-time attacks against machine learning models. Like other security research, it is dual-use: the techniques we describe could in principle be used by malicious actors, but we believe publication serves the broader goal of improving AI safety. By characterizing when influence-guided poisoning succeeds and fails, we provide actionable information for defenders designing data curation pipelines and training procedures. The attacks we demonstrate require white-box access to a proxy model and substantial computational resources, limiting immediate misuse potential. We hope this work motivates investment in data provenance, influence-based monitoring, and other defenses before such attacks become practical at scale.

\section{Supplementary Proofs}

\subsection{Perturbing a Document}
\label{apndx_perturbing}

Following a similar proof to \citet{koh2017understanding}, for a document $z$, we define $z_\delta = z + \delta$, and let $\hat{\theta}_{z_\delta,-z}$ be the empirical risk minimizer when $z$ is replaced with $z_\delta$.

To approximate its effects, define the parameters resulting from moving $\epsilon$ mass from $z$ onto $z_\delta$ by:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\theta}_{z_\delta,-z} = \arg \min_{\theta} \frac{1}{n} \sum^n_{i = 1} L(z_i, \theta) + \epsilon L(z_\delta, \theta) - \epsilon L (z,\theta)
\label{eq:pert1}
\end{equation}
A classic result from \citet{cook1982residuals} is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z) \overset{\text{def}}{=} \left. \frac{d\hat{\theta}_{\epsilon,z}}{d\epsilon} \right|_{\epsilon=0} = - H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1} \nabla_{\theta} L(z, \hat{\theta})
\end{equation}
and via the same derivation we get:
\begin{equation}
\left.\frac{d\hat{\theta}_{\epsilon,z_\delta, -z}}{d\epsilon} \right|_{\epsilon=0} =\mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z_\delta) - \mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z) =  - H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1} \big(\nabla_{\theta} L(z_\delta, \hat{\theta})-\nabla_{\theta} L(z, \hat{\theta})\big)
\end{equation}

We can make the linear approximation:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\hat\theta = \hat\theta_{z_\delta,-z} - \hat\theta \approx \frac{1}{n} \big(\mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z_\delta) - \mathcal{I}_{\text{up,params}}(z) \big)
\end{equation}


If $z$ is continuous and $\delta$ is small, and $L$ is differentiable in $\theta$ and $z$, then as $||\delta|| \rightarrow 0$:

\begin{equation}
\nabla_{\theta} L(z_\delta, \hat{\theta})-\nabla_{\theta} L(z, \hat{\theta}) \approx \big[ \nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z, \hat\theta)\big]\delta
\end{equation}
such that:
\begin{equation}
\Delta\hat\theta \approx -\frac{1}{n} H_{\hat{\theta}}^{-1} \big[ \nabla_z \nabla_\theta L(z, \hat\theta)\big]\delta
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Test 1: Increase in Target-Class Probability}
This test answers the fundamental question: \textit{Did the attack actually raise the probability of the target class?} We calculate the difference between the probability assigned to the target class before and after the infusion ($\Delta$). A positive mean of $0.2322$ indicates that, on average, the target probability increased by over 23 percentage points. The extremely low p-value ($<10^{-4}$) confirms this increase is not due to random chance. Additionally, the Cohen's $d$ value of $0.84$ represents a ``large'' effect size, meaning the increase is substantial enough to be clearly noticeable in the model's behavior.

\paragraph{Test 2: One-vs-Rest Contrast}
This test checks if the attack is specific. Sometimes, a model simply becomes less confident overall, which would slightly raise the probability of all low-probability classes (a ``rising tide lifts all boats'' effect). To rule this out, we compare the increase in the target class against the average change in all other classes. The positive mean contrast ($0.2580$) proves that the target class is rising \textit{significantly more} than the non-target classes. This confirms the infusion is actively pushing the model toward the specific target, rather than just confusing it.

\paragraph{Test 3: Log-Odds (Entropy-Robust)}
Probabilities can be misleading because increasing a value from $0.01$ to $0.10$ is much harder than increasing it from $0.40$ to $0.49$, even though the difference is the same. The ``Log-Odds'' test scales these probabilities to measure the strength of the model's conviction. This test shows the strongest result of all, with a Cohen's $d$ of $2.86$ (a massive effect size). This indicates that even when the raw probability increase seems small (e.g., on a very hard sample), the relative strength of the target class vs. the others has shifted dramatically in favor of the target.

\paragraph{Test 4: Permutation Test}
This test serves as a specificity check to confirm that the observed probability increases are only happening for the classes we intentionally targeted. It works by comparing the real result ($T_{\text{obs}}$) to a baseline generated by chance (the Null Distribution). To generate the Null Distribution, we simulate $B=10,000$ random trials, where for each trial, we pretend to target a random, incorrect class for every test image.
Our actual attack scored $T_{\text{obs}} = 464.39$. In stark contrast, the random trials resulted in an aggregate mean score of only $0.05$. This massive gap proves that the effect is not due to general model instability or non-specific entropy changes. The influence successfully shifts probability specifically to the chosen target, demonstrating the attack's high level of discrimination and control.

\paragraph{Test 5: Targeted Success Rate}
While the previous tests measure probability shifts, this test measures practical success: \textit{Did the target become the model's top prediction?} We compare the success rate before ($10\%$) and after ($37.35\%$) infusion. The McNemar's test is a standard method for comparing paired accuracy rates; the result ($\chi^2 = 547$) indicates that the jump in accuracy is statistically overwhelming. Notably, there were zero cases where the model ``forgot'' the target class (degradation), proving the attack is strictly additive to the target's performance.

\section{Document Selection Ablations}
\label{apndx_ablations}

We ablate the document selection strategy by comparing five approaches:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Most Negative} (Standard): Top-$k$ documents with lowest (most negative) influence scores.
    \item \textbf{Random}: $k$ randomly selected training documents.
    \item \textbf{Most Positive}: Top-$k$ documents with highest influence scores.
    \item \textbf{Most Absolute}: Top-$k$ documents by $|\text{influence}|$.
    \item \textbf{Last-$k$}: Last $k$ documents in training order.
\end{itemize}

\section{Retraining Duration Ablation}
\label{apndx_retrain}

We ablate the choice of retraining from epoch 9 (the standard Infusion setting) by testing different starting points. For each probe, we compute perturbations once using the epoch-10 model, then retrain from epochs 9, 8, 7, \ldots, 0 (full retrain from random initialization) on the infused dataset.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Epoch 9$\to$10} (1 epoch): Standard Infusion, minimal retraining.
    \item \textbf{Epoch 8$\to$10} (2 epochs): Slightly longer retraining window.
    \item \textbf{$\vdots$}
    \item \textbf{Epoch 0$\to$10} (10 epochs): Full training from scratch on infused data.
\end{itemize}

This ablation tests whether the infusion effect persists through extended training or is washed out as the model sees more gradient updates.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/retrain_ablation_boxplot.png}
    \caption{Effect of retraining duration on Infusion effectiveness. The x-axis shows the number of retraining epochs (1 = standard epoch 9$\to$10, 10 = full retrain from scratch). Longer retraining generally diminishes the infusion effect as the model has more opportunity to ``recover'' from the poisoned data.}
    \label{fig:retrain_ablation}
\end{figure}
